Abstract

20

Chromatin states, which are defined by specific combinations of histone post-

21

translational modifications, are fundamental to gene regulation and cellular identity. Despite

22

their importance, comprehensive patterns within chromatin state sequences, which could provide

23

insights into key biological functions, remain largely unexplored. In this study, we introduce

24

ChromBERT, a BERT-based model specifically designed to detect distinct chromatin state

25

patterns as “motifs.” We pre-trained ChromBERT on 15-state chromatin annotations from 127

26

human cell and tissue types from the ROADMAP consortium. This pre-trained model can be

27

fine-tuned for various downstream tasks, and obtained high-attention chromatin state patterns are

28

extracted as motifs. To account for the variable-length nature of chromatin state motifs,

29

ChromBERT uses Dynamic Time Warping to cluster similar motifs and identify meaningful

30

representative patterns. In this study, we evaluated the performance of the model on several

31

tasks, including binary and quantitative gene expression prediction, cell type classification, and

three-dimensional genome feature classification. Our analyses yielded biologically grounded

33

results and revealed the associated chromatin state motifs. This workflow facilitates the

34

discovery of specific chromatin state patterns across different biological contexts and offers a

35

new framework for exploring the dynamics of epigenomic states.

Background

38

The understanding of chromatin organization is essential to reveal the complex

39

mechanisms that govern gene regulation and function in the human genome[1], [2]. Chromatin

40

states, defined by the combined features of histone modifications, including the involvement of

41

DNA methylation and acetylation, are associated with specific functions such as gene activation,

42

repression, or structural organization [3], [4]. For instance, active chromatin states—often

43

marked by histone modifications such as H3K4me3 (trimethylation of lysine 4 on histone H3)

44

and H3K27ac (acetylation of lysine 27 on histone H3) —are generally linked to gene activation

45

and transcriptional initiation [5], [6]. These associations were further refined by Heintzman et

46

al.[7], who distinguished promoter- and enhancer-specific marks, and Creyghton et al.[8], who

47

identified H3K27ac as a marker of active regulatory elements. A deeper understanding of

48

chromatin states and their genomic distribution has been facilitated by the chromatin state

49

annotation tools such as ChromHMM [9] and Segway [10] coupled with the advent of chromatin

50

immunoprecipitation followed by high-throughput sequencing (ChIP-seq) experiments [11],

51

[12], [13]. Such annotation tools process genome-wide ChIP-seq datasets and systematically

52

learn and characterize combinatorial patterns of histone modifications into distinct chromatin

53

states, such as “active promoters,” “genic enhancers” and “heterochromatin.”

54

Large databases of epigenome information have been generated by international

55

consortia, such as the ROADMAP project [14], the ENCODE project [15], and the International

56

Human Epigenome Consortium (IHEC) [16]. For instance, the ROADMAP project characterized

57

and annotated the chromatin state of 127 distinct human cell and tissue types. The chromatin

58

state dataset can be used to perform various downstream analyses to decipher the hidden

59

intricacies of the chromatin state landscape. As one such attempt, ChromDiff [17] compared a

60

summarized set of chromatin states across different epigenomes (cell and tissue types), offering

61

new insights into tissue-specific or age-dependent chromatin alterations. More recently, CSREP

62

[18], a framework for deriving representative chromatin state maps for groups of samples, was

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.07.25.605219; this version posted March 17, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made
available under aCC-BY-NC 4.0 International license.

63

developed by Vu et al. Utilizing an ensemble of multi-class logistic regression classifiers, this

64

method enables the summarization of chromatin states for different groups of epigenomes with

65

high resolution. Additionally, Epilogos aims to visualize the conserved chromatin states across

66

different epigenomes based on the surprisal score measured from the rarity of the chromatin state

67

distribution [19]. A similar method, established with a different metric, the conservation-

68

associated activity score, was suggested by Libbrecht et al [20] as a novel annotation strategy

69

that has the advantage of detecting more biologically functional regions.

70

Recently, deep learning approaches designed for natural language processing have shown

71

great promise in deciphering complex biological patterns from large-scale genomic and

72

epigenomic datasets. Transformer-based architectures have been especially effective in modeling

73

sequential dependencies, leading to successful applications in biological contexts. For instance,

74

DNABERT [21] has been developed for DNA sequence analysis, while Geneformer [22] and

75

EPInformer [23] have demonstrated the potential of deep learning for modeling cellular states

76

and integrating epigenomic data.

77

The availability of chromatin state annotations for a large dataset, recent advances in

78

deep learning, and the precedent related studies have paved the way for the next important step:

79

identifying patterns of chromatin states as ‘motifs’. A ‘motif’ refers to a recurring sequence that

80

possesses a biological significance, often associated with specific functions such as binding sites

81

in DNA sequences [24]. In the context of our study, we extend this concept to chromatin states to

82

identify distinct patterns that could be key to understanding gene regulation. Instead of

83

examining individual genomic loci separately, analyzing the patterns and combinations of

84

chromatin states in relation to the surrounding regions, which we refer to as chromatin state

85

motifs, could reveal the functional significance and regulatory roles of spatially coordinated

86

epigenomic modifications that were previously unrecognized. By mapping these motifs, we may

87

begin to decode the epigenetic language that potentially modulates gene expression patterns

88

across different cell types and developmental stages. However, since chromatin state patterns are

89

inherently dynamic and varying in length and structure, they may not be fully captured by

90

traditional motif identification methods, such as k-mer-based algorithms, which often rely on

91

simplistic representations of DNA sequence [25], [26]. This underscores the need for strategies

92

that can accurately decipher the motifs of chromatin states and reveal their functional

93

implications in gene regulation and chromatin organization.

In this study, we introduce ChromBERT, which is specifically tailored for the discovery

95

of chromatin state motifs using the Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers

96

(BERT) model [27]. BERT has proven highly effective in natural language processing tasks and

97

provides an important way to analyze sequential patterns in biological data [28]. By integrating

98

the BERT model with the sequence alignment algorithm called Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)

99

[29], ChromBERT effectively analyzes dynamic chromatin states, which extends the concept

100

proposed by DNABERT for DNA sequence analysis [21]. ChromBERT is designed as a general-

101

purpose model capable of learning and representing genome-wide chromatin state patterns across

102

cell types. In this study, we evaluated the performance of ChromBERT on several tasks,

103

including binary and quantitative gene expression prediction, cell type classification, and three-

104

dimensional genome feature classification. Using the chromatin state annotations from 127

105

human cell and tissue types from the ROADMAP consortium, ChromBERT successfully

106

identified chromatin state motifs, demonstrating its potential for predicting more complex

107

genomic features. The source code of ChromBERT is available at

108

https://github.com/caocao0525/ChromBERT.

Results

111

ChromBERT framework

112

Figure 1 provides an overview of the ChromBERT framework, illustrating the key

113

components and workflow involved. We used the 15-state chromatin annotations defined by the

114

ROADMAP project, which are based on a combination of five histone modifications. Initially,

115

numerically annotated chromatin state labels ranging from 1 to 15 are converted into alphabetical

116

codes ranging from A to O, resulting in the generation of a chromatin state sequence (Figure

117

1(a)). In the training phase, the chromatin state sequences are tokenized and fed into a BERT

118

model for embedding (Figure 1(b)). We tokenized the chromatin state sequences into

119

overlapping 4-mers by sliding one character, resulting in a large vocabulary size of 50,630

120

including standard special tokens. We also explored tokenization by sliding N characters at once

121

(stride N, Supplementary Fig. S1). Since chromatin state sequences are more continuous than

122

DNA sequences, we assumed that skipping multiple characters would not significantly affect

123

prediction performance. This approach enables the use of longer input sequences and allows the

124

model to learn long-range dependencies across regulatory regions.

125

The overall workflow of ChromBERT is captured in Figure 1(c), starting with the

126

preprocessing of chromatin state sequence data. ChromBERT learns the general patterns of

127

chromatin state placement through a pretraining stage. Following this, ChromBERT is fine-tuned

128

for specific binary classification and quantitative regression tasks, such as promoter region

129

classification and gene expression level prediction. As a downstream analysis of the

130

classification task, chromatin state motifs specific to each task are extracted from the attention

131

matrix values.

132

Motif discovery is a key downstream component of ChromBERT. Unlike DNA sequence

133

motifs, chromatin state motifs lack well-defined reference libraries, making their identification

134

and interpretation more challenging. Additionally, chromatin state sequences are inherently cell-

135

type specific and dynamic. Even chromatin state patterns with similar regulatory functions can

136

vary in length across genomic regions, reflecting both biological variability (e.g., differences in

137

regulatory domain size) and technical variability (e.g., fluctuations in ChIP-seq signal-to-noise

138

ratios). To address these challenges, ChromBERT employs a Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)-

139

based approach to align and cluster motifs based on their structural similarity rather than exact

140

sequence identity. This enables biologically meaningful motif discovery that accounts for the

141

variability in chromatin state patterns. See the Methods section for further technical details.

Pretraining ChromBERT

144

To learn generalizable representations of chromatin state sequences, ChromBERT was

145

first pretrained using a masked token prediction objective on chromatin state sequences across

146

127 human cell and tissue types in the ROADMAP dataset. We pretrained ChromBERT in two

147

ways for different use cases. First, ChromBERT was pretrained on promoter regions (from 2 kb

148

upstream to 4 kb downstream of transcription start sites [TSSs]), as illustrated in Figure 2(a).

149

These regions were chosen because they play a central role in gene regulation, integrating signals

150

from nearby and distal cis-regulatory elements such as enhancers, silencers, and insulators [30].

151

Second, we pretrained the model on chromatin state sequences spanning the entire genome to

152

support broader applications and facilitate flexible fine-tuning for other genomic regions or

153

tasks. In this study, we primarily used the whole-genome–pretrained model.

154

Throughout the pretraining process, the model’s perplexity decreased steadily, indicating

155

successful learning of chromatin sequence structure (Figure 2(b)). For genome-wide pretraining,

perplexity dropped from approximately 4.96 to 1.09. For promoter-specific pretraining, it

157

decreased from approximately 2.75 to 1.24. The relatively low initial perplexity, particularly in

158

promoter regions, likely reflects the repetitive and locally stable nature of chromatin state

159

patterns near regulatory elements. See Methods section for further technical details.

ChromBERT distinguishes between highly and lowly expressed genes based on chromatin

162

state patterns of promoter-proximal regions

163

To evaluate the ability of ChromBERT to capture regulatory features, we first fine-tuned

164

the model on chromatin state sequences around the TSSs to classify regions associated with

165

genes of different expression levels. We used 57 cell types for which RNA-seq data are available

166

in ROADMAP and evaluated the binary classification accuracy between highly expressed genes

167

and genes with different expression levels. Genes were grouped based on their log-transformed

168

expression values (RPKM). Genes with log-transformed RPKM values greater than 5 were

169

labeled as ‘highly expressed,’ and we compared them with five groups: non-expressed genes and

170

gene groups whose log-transformed RPKM values fell within 1–2, 2–3, 3–4, or 4–5. Figure 3(a)

171

shows representative chromatin state sequences in promoter regions associated with highly

172

expressed and non-expressed genes. Active states such as “active TSS” (“A”), “strong

173

transcription” (“D”), “weak transcription” (“E”), and “genic enhancers” (“F”) frequently appear

174

adjacent to highly expressed genes. In contrast, repressed states, such as Polycomb-associated

175

states (“M” and “N”), dominate non-expressed regions.

176

We next evaluated how the choice of input region around TSSs affects fine-tuning

177

performance. We tested multiple combinations of upstream and downstream windows and

178

summarized the resulting F1 scores in Figure 3(b) (AUC and accuracy shown in Supplementary

179

Fig. S2). As expected, classification between highly expressed and non-expressed genes was

180

consistently high across all input lengths, while comparisons against genes with higher

181

expression levels were progressively more challenging and yielded lower scores. Across input

182

configurations, we observed modest performance gains when upstream regions were extended

183

from short (50 kb) to moderately long ranges (approximately 90–190 kb). Including downstream

184

regions also improved performance, particularly when combined with longer upstream windows.

185

The highest overall accuracy was obtained with the up190k/down100k configuration (orange

186

arrow). These results indicate that promoter-proximal chromatin states over extended upstream

ChromBERT identifies promoter chromatin state motifs associated with high gene

193

expression

194

We next examined how ChromBERT distributes attention across the input sequences to

195

identify which regions contribute most strongly to the classification task. As shown in Figure

196

4(a) and 4(b), attention values were sharply concentrated around the TSS, indicating that

197

ChromBERT consistently prioritizes promoter-proximal features even when provided with

198

extended upstream and downstream contexts. Outside this central peak, we also observed smaller

199

high-attention regions on both sides of the TSS, suggesting that additional regulatory patterns in

200

nearby upstream and downstream regions contribute to the model’s decisions. These results

201

highlight that promoter-proximal chromatin states serve as the primary informative signals for

202

distinguishing expression levels, while adjacent regions provide supplementary contextual cues.

203

To illustrate how the model focuses on individual promoter-proximal sequences, we

204

visualized representative loci by plotting chromatin states with heights proportional to their

205

attention values (Figure 4(c)). In the highly expressed example, the region surrounding the TSS

206

shows high attention on promoter- and transcription-associated chromatin states, including

207

“active TSS” (“A”), “flanking active TSS” (“B”), “strong transcription” (“D”), “weak

208

transcription” (“E”), and with smaller contributions from “enhancers” (“G”). In a moderately

209

expressed gene, attention around the TSS is lower overall and shifts toward transcribed and

210

“ZNF genes + repeat” associated states (“H”), reflecting the chromatin context of this locus.

211

Together with the global distribution of attention values (Figure 4(d)), these examples suggest

212

that promoter- and transcription-associated states provide the primary signals for distinguishing

213

expression levels, while nearby enhancer or repeat-associated states supply additional contextual

214

information.

(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made

217

The previous results suggested that chromatin state sequences tend to be persistent.

218

Additionally, due to biological factors (e.g., different lengths of genes and enhancer regions) or

219

technical factors (e.g., differences in read depth and signal-to-noise ratios across ChIP-seq

220

samples), motif instances may vary in both character and length, even if they have the same

221

biological functions. To summarize overarching patterns among the variable-length instances, we

222

applied DTW and grouped similar chromatin state motifs into coherent families. This grouping

223

uncovers recurring regulatory structures across genomic contexts post-fine-tuning, while still

224

accounting for local flexibility in sequence patterns.

225

Initially, ChromBERT identifies motifs without imposing constraints on the merging.

226

This approach captures a comprehensive set of chromatin state patterns that potentially signify

227

the regions of interest, with selection based on statistical significance (p-value). ChromBERT

228

then uses DTW to align the similar motifs in the set. Originating from the field of speech

229

recognition, DTW is adept at accommodating variations in the tempo of analogous spoken words

230

[31], making it particularly suitable for our purposes of aligning the different lengths of

231

continuous sequences. DTW computes sequence similarities not by conventional point-to-point

232

Euclidean distances but by minimizing the cumulative distances between motifs. Finally, the

233

agglomerative clustering algorithm [32] is applied to cluster similar patterns into categories

234

based on the sequence similarities, utilizing the hierarchical merging of data points based on

235

their pairwise similarities.

236

The overall workflow of motif clustering is described in Figure 5(a). First, the pairwise

237

DTW scores of motifs are calculated in both forward-forward and forward-reverse manners

238

because the epigenomic patterns obtained by ChIP-seq do not distinguish between the forward

239

and reverse strands. The lower DTW score representing higher similarities is adopted. Clustered

240

motifs can be identified after determining the optimal number of clusters using a dendrogram. To

241

visualize the clustering results, Figure 5(b) shows a UMAP projection of the motifs in a reduced-

242

dimensional space [33]. This projection enables clearer assessment of how well the clusters are

243

separated and reveals the overall structure of the motif space learned by ChromBERT. Figure

244

5(c) provides a cluster-wise summary that visualizes the composition and relative size of each

245

cluster, allowing for intuitive interpretation of the distribution and prevalence of chromatin state

246

motif patterns across samples. Together, these visualizations help convey both the diversity and

247

consistency of motif types captured by ChromBERT. A line plot representation of all motif

250

To estimate the optimal number of clusters, we integrated several evaluative measures,

251

including the dendrogram generated by the agglomerative clustering algorithm, to achieve a

252

judicious balance between detail and overarching pattern recognition. In Figure 5(c), for

253

instance, motifs with similar state transitions but different lengths, such as “BBBBGGG”,

254

“BGGGGGG”, “GGGBBB”, were clustered into a single category, while “GGBAAAAAA,”

255

which has a different transition, was clearly segregated into a separate category. Such transitions

256

like “G-B-A” could represent enhancer regions immediately preceding active TSS regions,

257

potentially acting as a herald for transcription initiation. This suggests a dynamic regulatory

258

setup where enhancer activation might directly influence the onset of transcription at proximal

259

promoter sites. Additionally, sequences such as “OOAAA” and “OOOOOAAA” in other clusters

260

may reflect abrupt changes in chromatin states, possibly serving as precursors to promoter

261

regions. Such patterns suggest the potential complexity of chromatin regulation captured by

262

ChromBERT, and these motifs could provide interesting insights into the orchestrated events

263

leading up to gene expression.

ChromBERT predicts gene expression levels from promoter chromatin state sequences
To further expand ChromBERT’s utility, we adapted the model for regression tasks and

267

attempted to predict gene expression levels quantitatively from promoter-proximal chromatin

268

state sequences. The model was fine-tuned using log-transformed RPKM values associated with

269

each gene.

270

We tested multiple combinations of upstream and downstream input lengths around TSSs

271

(Figure 6a). Consistent with the binary classification, performance improved when both

272

upstream and downstream regions were extended. The best configuration (100 kb upstream and

273

90 kb downstream) achieved a Pearson correlation of 0.791 between predicted and observed log-

274

transformed RPKM values (Figure 6(b)), indicating that ChromBERT can quantitatively predict

275

gene expression levels from chromatin state sequences. As illustrated in Supplementary Fig. S5,

276

the categorical nature of chromatin state data leads to cases where similar sequence patterns

277

exhibit different expression levels, which may constrain the maximum attainable correlation.

ChromBERT identifies cell-type–specific chromatin state signatures

286

To further evaluate the effectiveness of ChromBERT, we examined whether chromatin

287

state sequences contain recognizable signatures of different cell-type groups and whether the

288

model can classify them. We focused on cis-regulatory module (CRM) regions, which are

289

enriched for regulatory activity. CRM regions were obtained from the SCREEN database [34],

290

and chromatin state sequences were extracted from 20-kb windows centered on each CRM site.

291

Cell-type group annotations followed the ROADMAP classification (“ESC,” “iPSC,” “ES-

292

derived,” “blood-Tcell,” “HSC-Bcell,” “Brain,” “Muscle,” “Heart,” and “Smooth Muscle”), and

293

we fine-tuned ChromBERT for binary classification across all pairwise combinations (Figure

294

7a).

295

The resulting accuracy distribution closely reflected known biological relationships

296

among cell groups. For example, ESCs and iPSCs are highly similar in their epigenomic profiles

297

and accordingly were difficult to distinguish (0.655). In contrast, ESCs and T cells showed more

298

distinct chromatin patterns and were classified with higher accuracy (0.776). These results

299

suggest that each cell group carries characteristic epigenomic signatures. One exception was the

300

Brain vs. Smooth Muscle comparison, which had low accuracy (0.529). One possible reason is a

301

problem with the data quality. According to the ROADMAP QC summary, multiple histone-

302

modification ChIP-seq samples for these two cell groups were flagged as low quality[14]. These

303

findings are consistent with those from the TSS-based gene-expression classification task (see

304

Supplementary Fig. S4).

305

We next sought to identify motifs contributing to cell-type specificity. Figure 7(b) shows

306

motif clusters enriched in ESCs when compared with T-cells. Interestingly, many ESC-specific

307

motifs contain the character “J,” corresponding to the “bivalent/poised TSS” state, a hallmark of

308

pluripotent stem cells [35], [36]. Motifs containing “J” appeared in in ESCs and iPSCs but not in

differentiated cell types. A complete list of identified motifs is provided in Supplementary Table

However, ChromBERT also identified motifs that appeared cell-type-unspecific (e.g.,

ChromBERT captures chromatin state signatures of 3D genome compartments

318

Lastly, we evaluated whether ChromBERT could capture features associated with three-

319

dimensional (3D) genome organization. Although the relationship between the epigenome and

320

3D genome structure is still not well understood, if ChromBERT can accurately predict certain

321

3D genome features, it would suggest a strong correspondence between epigenomic states and

322

higher-order genome organization. Using in situ Hi-C data from five cell types (GM12878,

323

HMEC, HUVEC, IMR90, and K562) at 25-kb resolution obtained from Rao et al. [37], we first

324

examined whether chromatin state sequences could distinguish A/B compartments (Figure 8(a)).

325

See the Methods section for details on the Hi-C analysis. Compartments were defined using the

326

first principal component (PC1) of the observed/expected contact matrix, where positive and

327

negative PC1 values correspond to compartment A (generally active) and compartment B

328

(generally inactive), respectively [38]. We additionally defined “strong compartment A” and

329

“strong compartment B” as genomic bins with extreme PC1 values. As expected from their

330

distinct chromatin compositions, ChromBERT accurately classified compartments A and B, with

331

even higher performance for the strong A/B subsets where epigenomic differences were more

332

pronounced.

333

We next attempted to classify topologically associating domain (TAD) boundaries versus

334

non-TAD boundary regions (Figure 8(b)). TAD boundaries were identified from Hi-C contact

335

maps following standard insulation-score–based procedures [39]. These regions are known to be

336

enriched for the architectural protein CTCF and for housekeeping genes [40], suggesting that

337

characteristic chromatin states might help distinguish them. However, unlike the compartment

338

task, classification accuracy for TAD boundary versus non-boundary regions was modest (F1 <

339

0.7), and extending the input window to 200 kb resulted in only limited improvement. Several

Discussion

354

ChromBERT is a Transformer-based architecture specifically designed for chromatin

355

state sequences. Unlike DNA sequence models such as DNABERT, which operate on static

356

nucleotide sequences, ChromBERT learns from epigenomic annotations that are cell-type–

357

specific and inherently variable in length. While recent deep learning approaches such as

358

Geneformer [22] and EPInformer [23] have modeled regulatory states or integrated multi-omics

359

signals, ChromBERT provides a complementary perspective by learning sequential patterns

360

directly from chromatin state annotation data. By leveraging self-attention mechanisms,

361

ChromBERT learns recurrent chromatin state motifs directly from sequence-level data, capturing

362

structural patterns that are not the focus of annotation summarization tools like CSREP [18],

363

alignment-based methods such as EpiAlign [41], or the conservation-based approach like

364

Epilogos and the conservation-associated activity score [20]. This sequence-level viewpoint

365

offers a data-driven framework for studying local epigenomic organization and its relationship to

366

transcriptional regulation.

367

Across multiple downstream tasks, including gene expression prediction, cell-type

368

classification, and the prediction of selected 3D genome features, ChromBERT demonstrated

369

that chromatin state sequences encode biologically meaningful patterns. For example, promoter-

370

proximal chromatin states enabled accurate binary and quantitative gene expression prediction,

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.07.25.605219; this version posted March 17, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made
available under aCC-BY-NC 4.0 International license.

371

and cell-type–specific CRM regions yielded signatures consistent with known epigenomic

372

programs (e.g., enrichment of the bivalent/poised TSS state “J” in pluripotent cells [35], [36]).

373

Chromatin states also captured large-scale 3D genome organization: A/B compartments were

374

readily classified, with stronger compartment regions showing particularly high separability.

375

These results support the idea that chromatin state annotations retain regulatory information that

376

can be learned directly at the sequence level.

377

A notable strength of ChromBERT is its scalability with respect to pretraining data.

378

Because chromatin state sequences differ across cell types, the number of available training

379

samples naturally increases as more cell types are incorporated. We also pretrained ChromBERT

380

using chromatin annotation data from the IHEC project [16], which provides 1,699 epigenome

381

annotations based on an 18-state model. Once the IHEC database becomes publicly available, the

382

corresponding pretrained model will also be released. However, increased data volume also

383

magnifies technical variation. Public ChIP-seq datasets vary in quality, and including samples

384

with weak signal-to-noise ratios may introduce noise during pretraining. Similarly, many cell

385

types are represented by multiple donors or slightly modified conditions, potentially leading to

386

imbalanced datasets. Careful data curation and quality filtering will therefore be essential for

387

maximizing model performance.

388

From a computational perspective, chromatin state modeling introduces unique

389

challenges. DNABERT’s nucleotide alphabet consists of only four bases, resulting in

390

manageable k-mer vocabularies (e.g., 4,101 (6-mers)). In contrast, ChromBERT must represent

391

15 chromatin states, and the resulting k-mer vocabularies expand sharply in absolute size: 3,379

392

(3-mers), 50,629 (4-mers), 759,379 (5-mers), and over 11 million (6-mers). This pronounced

393

difference in vocabulary size is computationally challenging and can lead GPU memory

394

limitations when attempting to pretrain with more than 5-mers, even with a single sample set.

395

This effectively limited us to using 4-mers for the pre-training step. The vocabulary complexity

396

becomes even more substantial when using the 18-state chromatin annotation system from the

397

IHEC dataset.

398

Additionally, the perplexity of the model (a metric that quantifies how well a model

399

predicts a sample) showed a decreasing trend from approximately 4 to 1 over iterations during

400

the pretraining for both promoter regions and broader genic regions. Interestingly, the perplexity

401

was already relatively low at the beginning of training (approx. 4). This suggests that the model

(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made
available under aCC-BY-NC 4.0 International license.

402

is successfully predicting the masked tokens in the pretraining, even in the early stages. This

403

initially low perplexity can be attributed to several factors. First, the stable, long-lasting nature of

404

chromatin states and their functional bias likely lead to more predictable sequence patterns,

405

easing the model’s predictions. Second, our use of 4-mer tokenization further simplified the task

406

for the model, as it only needs to predict patterns within a more limited context compared to

407

larger k-mers.

408

ChromBERT achieved strong predictive performance across multiple tasks. In gene

409

expression regression, the model reached a Pearson’s r of 0.79 and captured promoter-proximal

410

regulatory patterns that reflect relative transcriptional activity. Extending the promoter window

411

improved performance up to a point, indicating that distal regulatory features contribute

412

additional information. Chromatin state sequences also facilitated the discovery of recurring

413

regulatory motifs, as their categorical representation integrates multiple histone marks and

414

enables long effective input windows.

415

Like other BERT-based architectures, ChromBERT is subject to inherent input-length

416

constraints, limiting the maximum sequence to 510 tokens. However, the locally stable and

417

consecutive nature of chromatin states allows this constraint to be partially mitigated through

418

stride-based tokenization, which enables the model to incorporate a broader genomic context

419

while maintaining meaningful patterns. In addition, while 4-mer tokenization during pretraining

420

does not directly restrict the motif length, its contextual granularity may bias the model toward

421

learning patterns on a similar scale. To detect longer motifs, it may be useful to modify the

422

sequence length constraints or revise the tokenization process to better accommodate the unique

423

properties of chromatin state annotation data. Such efforts could further improve ChromBERT’s

424

utility in decoding the complex, hierarchical nature of epigenomic regulation.

425

Unlike previous methods that use regression-based models to directly predict gene

426

expression from raw histone-modification signals, chromatin state data does not use peak-

427

intensity information. This could result in reduced performance for highly localized predictions.

428

However, chromatin states offer a simplified sequence representation that integrates multiple

429

histone marks, enabling much longer input sequences and allowing the model to focus on the

430

length and combinatorial patterns of state transitions. This representation enables the detection of

431

recurring regulatory patterns across samples and facilitates the discovery of sequence-level

432

chromatin motifs that generalize across cell types. Furthermore, this framework is not limited to

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.07.25.605219; this version posted March 17, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made
available under aCC-BY-NC 4.0 International license.

433

gene expression prediction. It can also serve as a foundation for many downstream tasks, such as

434

cell type classification and 3D genome-related analyses.

435

Performance for TAD-boundary prediction was modest, likely reflecting both technical

436

and biological factors. TAD boundaries are relatively few per cell type, vary by caller, and can

437

contain mixtures of active and inactive chromatin states, reducing separability. Incorporating

438

additional modalities, such as CTCF binding [42] or more finely resolved regulatory annotations,

439

may improve the model’s ability to identify finer-scale 3D architectural features as multi-omics

440

datasets expand.

441

Future work may explore model architectures with extended context, hierarchical

442

tokenization strategies, or dynamically learned segmentation to better capture long-range

443

epigenomic organization. Improved curation of input datasets, inclusion of additional cell types,

444

and integration of complementary epigenomic modalities may further enhance ChromBERT’s

445

generalizability.

446

In summary, ChromBERT provides a scalable, interpretable framework for learning

447

sequence-level patterns within chromatin state annotations. By capturing recurrent motifs,

448

promoter activity signatures, and compartment-level structures, ChromBERT illustrates how

449

language-model approaches can decode the structured complexity of the epigenome and link

450

local chromatin organization to diverse functional genomic outcomes.

1. Data collection

455

456

458

459

Ends, 4. Strong Transcription, 5. Weak Transcription, 6. Genic Enhancers, 7. Enhancers, 8. Zinc

460

Finger Protein (ZNF) Genes & Repeats, 9. Heterochromatin, 10. Bivalent/Poised TSS, 11.

461

Flanking Bivalent TSS/Enhancer, 12. Bivalent Enhancer, 13. Repressed Polycomb, 14. Weak

462

Repressed Polycomb, and 15. Quiescent and low signal. The chromatin annotation BED files

consist of four columns: chromosome number, start position, end position, and the number

464

2. Data preprocessing
To prepare the data for natural language processing techniques, we first converted the

468

chromatin-annotated data, originally represented by numbers 1 to 15, into corresponding

469

alphabetic characters A to O. This transformation was necessary because natural language

470

processing cannot be directly applied to sequences of numerical chromatin states. After changing

471

the chromatin state annotations to alphabetic symbols, we condensed the genomic positions by a

472

factor of 200 since the original annotations were conducted at 200-bp intervals. Consequently,

473

we generated a continuous string of chromatin states, with each alphabet character (A to O)

474

representing the corresponding chromatin state (1 to 15) for a 200-bp segment. Additionally, to

475

focus our analysis on the relevant genomic regions and to exclude potential biases from

476

telomeres, we removed the initial and final 10,000 bps from each chromosome, as these regions

477

generally represent the extreme ends and may not accurately reflect the overall chromatin states.

478

In this manner, all the chromatin annotation data for 127 epigenomes were processed

479

chromosome-wise. Since we adapted BERT architecture, the processed chromatin annotation

480

data were randomly cut to ensure that the length of the data did not exceed 510 but was longer

481

than 5, following their data preparation method. Subsequently, the chromatin state sequences

482

were tokenized into 3-mers, 4-mers, 5-mers, and 6-mers. Any sequences shorter than the length

483

of each k-mer were not included in the dataset.

3. Pretraining

486

The BERT architecture utilized in ChromBERT was pre-trained on chromatin annotation

487

data using datasets from 127 different cell types provided by the ROADMAP project for the 15

488

chromatin state case. The chromatin annotation sequences for the genic regions of these

489

epigenomes were preprocessed as described in the data processing section, concatenated, and

490

then tokenized using k-merization. Unlike DNABERT, we focused on testing 3-mers and 4-mers

491

only, as 5-mers and 6-mers have a significantly larger vocabulary size (759,380 and 11,390,630,

492

respectively), making them impractical to run. The vocabulary sizes for 3-mers and 4-mers were

493

3,379 and 50,630, respectively. Although both 3-mers and 4-mers demonstrated sufficiently low

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.07.25.605219; this version posted March 17, 2026. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made
available under aCC-BY-NC 4.0 International license.

494

perplexity, we opted for 4-mers because they comparably capture more complex patterns more

495

effectively, enhancing our analysis. The transformer architecture used for pretraining remained

496

consistent with DNABERT, featuring an intermediate layer size of 3,072 and a hidden layer size

497

of 768. To ensure smooth training without encountering out-of-memory errors, we adjusted the

498

number of train and evaluation batches per GPU to 5 and 3, respectively. For other training

499

conditions, we followed the same strategy as DNABERT, including a linear increase in the

500

learning rate from 0 to 4e-4 during the warm-up phase. The pretraining process was conducted

501

on a GPU machine equipped with two Nvidia GeForce RTX 2080Ti cards, and it took

502

approximately three days to complete.

503

For the IHEC dataset, chromatin state annotations consist of 18 states (A–R) defined at

504

200-bp resolution. These sequences were converted into 4-mers to construct the pretraining

505

corpus, yielding approximately 41 GB of data after removing tokens composed entirely of the

506

quiescent state R. Because the full dataset could not be loaded into memory at once, we adopted

507

a sequential pretraining strategy. The data were first randomly shuffled to distribute cell-line–

508

specific patterns uniformly, then split into chunks of 500,000 lines. Each chunk was used in

509

succession, with model weights carried over between chunks, enabling efficient training while

510

avoiding overfitting to contiguous regions from individual cell lines. Pretraining was performed

511

using a DNA-based Transformer architecture with 4-mer tokenization and a vocabulary of

512

104,980 tokens, representing all valid 4-mers from the 18-state system (excluding the RRRR

513

token) together with the standard special tokens ([UNK], [PAD], [CLS], [SEP], [MASK]).

514

During pretraining, we used a masked language modeling (MLM) objective, following

515

the standard BERT pretraining strategy. Specifically, 15% of tokens were randomly masked, as

516

in DNABERT, and the model was trained to predict these masked tokens using surrounding

517

context. Perplexity was used as the primary evaluation metric to assess learning performance.

518

While chromatin state sequences exhibit structured dependencies, the self-attention mechanism

519

enables ChromBERT to effectively capture both local and long-range relationships. Gradient

520

accumulation and a carefully chosen batch size were used to efficiently manage GPU memory

521

and allow training on large datasets. Model weights generated after each chunk were saved and

522

used as initialization for subsequent chunks, progressively refining the model across the dataset.

523

The training was conducted on a system equipped with an AMD EPYC 7513 32-Core Processor,

524

251 GiB of RAM, and two NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada GPUs, which were instrumental in handling

4. Fine-tuning for gene expression level classification
We conducted fine-tuning for each downstream application based on the parameters

533

acquired from the pretraining process. ChromBERT follows the BERT-base architecture,

534

consisting of 12 transformer layers, 12 attention heads, and a hidden size of 384. The

535

intermediate layer size was 1536, and we applied the GELU activation function. The model uses

536

a vocabulary size of 50,630, corresponding to all possible 4-mers derived from chromatin state

537

sequences. The maximum sequence length was set to 512, with dropout probabilities of 0.1 for

538

attention and hidden layers.

539

We followed the fine-tuning conditions used in DNABERT, applying AdamW as the

540

optimization method with a weight decay of 0.01. Fine-tuning was conducted using a batch size

541

of 32, a learning rate of 2e-4, and a warm-up proportion of 10% of the total training steps. Each

542

fine-tuning task was trained for 10 epochs, with evaluation performed every 100 steps. Model

543

checkpoints were saved every 4000 steps, and gradient accumulation was used to efficiently train

544

on large-scale datasets.

545

For pairwise binary classification across different gene expression levels, we utilized

546

RNA-seq data from 57 different cell lines provided by the ROADMAP project. A genic region

547

was classified as ‘highly expressed’ if its log-transformed RPKM (Reads Per Kilobase per

548

Million mapped reads) was greater than 5, while genic regions with an RPKM value of 0 were

549

550

551

based on a preliminary distribution analysis, and they resulted in more balanced gene counts

552

across classes.

555

637

638

8. Motif finding
Similarly to the definition of DNA motifs in DNABERT, chromatin state motifs in our

653

study are defined as frequently appearing patterns of consecutive chromatin states that are

654

distinctively present in a certain category during binary classification. We followed the

655

parameter conditions used in DNABERT to detect and filter these motifs. Firstly, we captured

656

regions with high attention scores from the output attention matrix. A threshold was defined as a

657

score higher than the mean attention scores of the matrix and higher than 10 times the minimum

658

score to determine regions where motifs are likely included. These regions were registered as

659

candidates, which were further filtered using a false discovery rate (FDR) threshold of 0.05,

660

based on a hypergeometric test followed by a Benjamini–Hochberg correction. This FDR

661

determined whether the motifs were significantly enriched in one class compared to the other.

662

Finally, motifs were identified as regions that are longer than four chromatin state sequences

663

(equivalent to 800 bps) and appear at least three times exclusively in the target class. While in

664

the case of DNA motifs, final motifs are typically compared with existing DNA motif libraries,

665

we collected chromatin state motifs without such a comparison because there is currently no

666

known library available for this task.

9. Motif clustering

669

To further estimate the representative motif chromatin sequence pattern, we utilized

670

DTW and agglomerative clustering to analyze the chromatin state motifs. Our initial dataset

671

included motifs identified after careful screening for p-values, minimum length, and minimum

672

occurrences within predefined regions of interest, avoiding the merging step to allow a detailed

673

examination of chromatin sequences, which are represented alphabetically. Given the potential

674

for variability in these sequences due to factors such as data quality (e.g., the signal-to-noise ratio

675

of the antibodies) or fluctuations in histone modification peaks, we opted for a more flexible

676

approach to motif identification. DTW was selected for its capability to adjust for sequence

677

length variations, accommodating the intrinsic dynamics of chromatin state sequences.

(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made

678

First, to implement DTW, we converted the chromatin state alphabets (“A” to “O”) into

679

numerical values (1 to 15) to facilitate a standardized analysis. We provide two options for

680

handling this numerical translation of chromatin states by offering a ‘categorical’ parameter,

681

which can be set to ‘True’ or ‘False’. If users set it to ‘True’, the numbers are considered

682

categorical, meaning the states A to B and A to O are equally distant. On the other hand, if it is

683

set to ‘False’, which is the default option, the numbers representing the states are considered

684

numerically; thus, the distance from state A to C is twice that of the distance from state A to B.

685

The result of motif clustering when the ‘categorical’ parameter is set to ‘True’ is as shown in

686

Supplementary Figure S8. Next, Shorter sequences were padded with ‘NaN’ (Not a Number) to

687

match the maximum length dictated by the window size, ensuring consistency across all entries.

688

For DTW calculation, the NaN values in shorter sequences are, by default, converted to the

689

number representing the nearest state according to the 'ffill' filling method. Since chromatin state

690

annotations are typically strand-agnostic and assigned without reference to gene orientation, we

691

considered both forward–forward and forward–reverse comparisons to account for potential

692

reverse-orientation patterns in chromatin state motifs. Consequently, the DTW matrix was

693

composed of the minimum score between these two comparisons. Using the ‘tslearn’ Python

694

package, particularly the ‘dtw’ module, we calculated similarity scores between sequences by

695

focusing on the minimum cumulative distances in both directions, allowing for a detailed

696

comparison of motifs.

697

Following this similarity assessment, we employed agglomerative clustering via the

698

‘AgglomerativeClustering’ module from the same package, grouping the motifs into clusters

699

based on their similarities. Additionally, we provide a function to generate a dendrogram, which

700

displays the hierarchy and helps users determine the optimal number of clusters when the

701

number of clusters is not predetermined. While the dendrogram was our primary tool for

702

selecting the optimal number of clusters, users are encouraged to consider alternative methods

703

for determining the cluster count. For visualization purposes, we provide two functions: the first

704

displays the UMAP, which reduces the dimensionality of the data for easier interpretation and

705

visualization of the clustering results; the second visualizes the final result of clustering with

706

actual motif entries. This approach facilitates the identification of representative sequential

707

patterns among chromatin states, thereby offering deeper insights into genomic regulatory

708

landscapes.
